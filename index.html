Introduction

This site aims to give an introduction to the fundamentals of information, computer, and network security. Firstly, in the security world there are a lot of buzzwords which may seem to relate to the same thing, but in reality can be very different depending on who you ask as there are multiple organisations that set these definitions.

Key Definitions

  • Information Security - The protection of information and information systems from unauthorized access, use, disclosure, disruption, modification, or destruction in order to provide confidentiality, integrity, and availability.
  • Cyber Security Measures and controls that ensure confidentiality, integrity, and availability of the information processed and stored by a computer.
  • Computer Security - Prevention of damage to, protection of, and restoration of computers, electronic communications systems, electronic communications services, wire communication, and electronic communication, including information contained therein, to ensure its availability, integrity, authentication, confidentiality, and nonrepudiation.
  • Confidentiality - Preserving authorized restrictions on access and disclosure, including means for protecting personal privacy and proprietary information.
  • Integrity - Guarding against improper information modification or destruction, and includes ensuring information non-repudiation and authenticity.
  • Availability - Ensuring timely and reliable access to and use of information.
  • Non-repudiation - A service that is used to ensure the integrity and origin of data in such a way that the integrity and origin can be verified and validated by a third party as having originated from a specific entity.
  • IoT - Stands for Internet of Things. Used to describe any device that is connected to a network and relies on interactions with it's environment, for example a smart thermostat. Also called Internet of Trash due to the terrible security features these devices usually have.
  • Cryptography - Branch of mathematics which focuses on obscuring information from people/ organisations who shouldn't be able to see said information through the use of complex algorithms.
  • APT - Acronym for advance persistent threat. A group/ organisation which use advanced hacking techniques to violate the security systems of their targets. Usually backed by governments and target key industries such as banking, healthcare and military.
  • Script kiddie - An individual with a low skill set and little financial backing who uses existing scripts to exploit existing vulnerabilities.
  • Firewall - A device that monitors and filters "traffic" through the use detection systems that analyse and react to specific patterns.

All of the terms above are interlinked and the meaning of them depends on who you ask as there are multiple organisations which set the definitions of these terms. Information Security will usually include the non-technical side of security as well, whereas Cybersecurity will not. Computer security is essentially Cybersecurity but on a smaller scale.

All defintions taken from NIST which is one of main organisations which creates standards, guidelines and best practices to manage Cybersecurity/ Information Security risks. The other organisation being the ISO, hence the difference in definitions and best practices amongst other things.

Boring bit over... for now.

Threats

Threat (NIST) - Any circumstance or event with the potential to adversely impact agency operations (including mission, functions, image, or reputation), agency assets, or individuals through an information system via unauthorized access, destruction, disclosure, modification of information, and/or denial of service.

The reasons, techniques and perpetrators of cyber crime vary massively ranging from revenge, financial gain, espionage or simply just for fun and are carried out by individuals, organisations and nations with varying degrees of damage.

Types of Attacks

  • Phishing
    • What is it - A form of social engineering where the target is tricked into performing an action which leads to a vulnerability.
    • How is it carried out - The attacker will usually send out emails or messages which appear to be genuine, such as a fake bank statement. This deceitful email will then trick the user into entering their bank details which are then transferred back to the attacker, allowing them to steal funds from the account.
    • How can it be prevented - Being aware of phishing attacks and taking care when clicking on links in emails/ messages is the best prevention technique here. Organisations may also introduce filters into their email system which restricts emails coming from outside sources.
  • Dos and DDoS
    • What is it - DoS and DDoS are both acronyms for Denial of Service and Distributed Denial of Service respectively. Usually directed towards organisations and governments instead of individuals as a result of ideology instead of financial gain.

    • How is it carried out - Both are similar as they both overload a server with requests, resulting in reduced functionality for that server and potentially shutting it down, hence DoS. DDoS attacks come from a network of connected computers called botnets whereas DoS attacks originate from one source. DDoS attacks are usually more punishing because of this as it can be difficult to find the origin and block it from making requests to a server.
    • How can it be prevented - By using a combination of Intrusion Detection Systems and Intrusion Prevention Systems. These monitor and block any unwanted connections by matching patterns between what is happening and what they have been “told” what a danger is.
  • Malware Attack
    • What is it - Generic term for any attack involving malicious software. Examples include key-loggers which track what you have typed and worms which replicate themselves and infect inter-connected devices as opposed to a regular virus which usually only infect one machine.
    • How is it carried out - Usually, there is an element of social engineering involved such as a phishing attack with a seemingly legitimate looking link which contains a malicious payload. Another way in which this is achieved is through Cross Site Scripting (XSS). To achieve this attackers will take advantage of an insecure website and sneakily embed the malicious software, which is then executed when a user accesses that website.
    • How can it be prevented - Some overlap with preventing phishing as social engineering is a key part in the delivery of malware so awareness of the way in which attackers operate is key. Anti-virus software is also a good idea. Some organisations impose restrictions on the devices their employees use. This is known as System Hardening
  • Brute Force Attack
    • What is it - A way in which an attacker “cracks” a password in order to gain unauthorized access to a system.
    • How is it carried out - Achieved through a program which normally submits between 10,000 and 1 billion passwords per second, depending on how powerful their equipment is. Simple to do and very effective.
    • How can it be prevented - Using very strong passwords i.e. long, not an actual word, and containing a mix of numbers, symbols and letters. However, even if a password is cracked, unauthorized access can still be prevented by implementing two-factor authentication (2FA), which requires the user to enter a second factor, typically a randomly generated number, when logging into a computer or service.
  • SQL Injection
    • What is it SQL is an acronym for structured query language and is usually used to gather and manipulate databases. SQL Injection or SQLI is used to retrieve data such as passwords and other credentials.
    • How is it carried out - When there is a combination of websites with weak security and a place where text can be inserted e.g. a login page an SQL command can be injected which either, retrieves, deletes or manipulates data in the targetted database.
    • How can it be prevented - Can be prevented by placing restrictions on input fields, creating a whitelist of permitted SQL commands and maintain up-to-date software if possible.
  • Packet Sniffing
    • What is it A technique in which a hacker uses a packet sniffing tool to gather information on the flow of data on a network
    • How is it carried out - Fairly simple to do and can be done at home. Simply download wireshark or some other Packet sniffing software and away you go.
    • How can it be prevented - The use of a VPN and only visiting websites that start with HTTPS would be advisable.

Mitigation and Security Controls

Mitigation (NIST) - A decision, action, or practice intended to reduce the level of risk associated with one or more threat events, threat scenarios, or vulnerabilities.

The global cyber security industry is spreading like a virus with no signs of slowing down, and according to these people these people, total spending on Cybersecurity is set to reach $1.75 trillion between 2021 and 2025.

Mitigation For Businesses

Although there will be some slight variation from industry to industry due to regulations they are expected to follow there is a general approach that most companies choose to take. These can be divided into human/ personnel and technical factors.

Personnel and Policy Security Measures

The human error side is an incredibly vulnerable side of cyber security, with some organisations having millions of employees it is not realistic to expect perfection. There are methods which exist to reduce vulnerabilities introduced.

Firstly, all employees need to be made aware of the risks the business faces and how attacks are typically carried out. As covered previously, attackers can perform spear phishing attacks to either gain access to a system or to spread malware. If there is not an effective policy in place, outlining staff training on the cyber security risks the onus cannot be on the employee. Other policies implemented to mitigate risk may include a clean desk policy i.e. no confidential information out in the open.

Secondly, there should be policies in place detailing an incident response plan which should go outline the following steps...

Finally, during the hiring process, any potential employees should be subjected to a screening process. This can vary from a basic background check in order to confirm their identity, to an invasive investigation of their personal life. This would probably include financial checks to ensure they are not at risk of selling company information to pay off debts, or making sure that they don't participate in activities that could make them vulnerable to blackmail.

  • Preparation Performing drills, policy reviews, staff training
  • Identification Time of attack, business areas affected(not usually physical locations, but possible), who discovered it.
  • Containment Making sure the attack doesn't spread to other business areas. This may necessitate taking certain services offline.
  • Eradication Removing any potentially infected machines. Does the whole system need to be replaced?
  • Recovery Returning affected business areas to a trustworthy state.
  • Lessons Learned Discussion on what needs to change so this type of attack won't happen again.

Technical Security Measures

The technical side of cybersecurity is, in my opinion, the most important aspect as personnel and policy security measures are essentially useless without it. Technical security measures provide protection from outside attacks and if employees behaved in a "perfect way" there would be no need for the personnel and policy security measures.

Say a new company has got to the stage where they are thinking about protecting their business and data from attacks the minimum requirements should include:

  1. Deciding who can access what data and resources both internally and externally through the use of access controls. This helps reduce the unwanted (whether malicious or not) creation, reading, updating, and deletion of system resources such as data or key software. For example, the payroll team should be the only people who can access the banking information and salaries of employees.

    There is some crossover here with the non-technical aspect as there may be physical access controls in place that restricts the movement of guests and different types of employees within an office for example.

  2. Making sure all software and operating systems the business is using is up to date as most of these updates include security patches making them less vulnerable to attacks. It may also be a good idea to implement a process called system hardening which implements restrictions on what tasks a specific user can do e.g. not allowing downloads of software or documents which aren't preapproved.

  3. Identify what areas are at risk and how an attack may be delivered. In most cases, this will be the data which is held by the business such personal banking information and the areas which an attacker will make their first point of contact e.g. email.

    There are three main methods to protect the data and databases involve.

    1. Encrypting the data held in a database is a good start, because even if an attacker manages to retrieve data from a database they will probably not be able to understand what the data is and how to decrypt it unless they have an advanced skill set or have insider information.   4. Create a barrier between the business' computing resources and the outside world world through the use of firewalls. Cybersecurity version of border control.

    A firewall monitors and potentially blocks incoming and outgoing network traffic through the use of tools such as Intrusion Prevention Systems and Intrusion Detection Systems, which are preprogrammed to recognise the patterns in which malicious activity occurs. The deeper you go into the firewall the more sophisticated the "tests". This saves time and therefore money as these more refined tests take more time, and if a request is obviously malicious there is no point using a time-expensive model when a basic model achieves the same result. This also reduces the likelihood of Dos/DDoS attacks.

Personal Mitigation

It is fairly easy, although mildly inconvenient to drastically improve your own security when using modern computing devices.

  1. Improving password implementation. This would involve not using the same password for every account, as if one password is leaked in a data breach, all of your accounts are vulnerable as well. This also includes setting strong passwords, what you want to do here is create a password with a high level of entropy (in this context it means the level of unpredicitability of the combination of characters in the password). The average password strength is around 40 bits of entropy, and therefore the maximum number of guesses required to crack it will be 2^40 attempts which may seem like a lot but can be cracked in under 30 minutes.

    For example XSgY5Fu has 41.65 bits of entropy so just above the average and can be cracked in around 17 minutes. 9ibU10BZ&X/RfYq has 98.25 bits of entropy and will take centuries to crack using current technology.

    Setting up two factor authentication in conjunction with a strong password would make you very secure online.

    bitwarden for password strength calculation, Ultra calculator for generating and calculating entropy of password, wikipedia for password strength

  2. It is highly recommended that you avoid using public Wi-Fi networks whenever possible, as they can pose significant security risks and if you have to, use a VPN. Connecting to public Wi-FI is extremely risky behaviour, and I would suggest that you never do it. An attacker is able to commit many different types of attacks such as a MITM attack in order to see any data sent and recieved such as passwords or delivering malware by altering the data that you are recieving.

  3. Keep all software and operating systems up to date. This just ensures that any known security vulnerabilities have been patched. For example, if you are still running Windows XP you are leaving yourself vulnerable to 71 known vulnerabilities.

    CVE Details: Windows XP

  4. Don't open texts and emails from unknown senders. More often than not these will be phishing attempts to try and trick you into handing over your personal data or downloading malware.
  5. Don't connect to websites which don't use HTTPS. By not using HTTPS sites you are not offered the protection offered by encryption via TLS allowing attackers to read all the information you are sending and receiving, as well as inserting malware into the sites.
  6. Don't save banking information when possible. This leaves your money at risk if a company you use falls victim to a data breach.

Privacy Concerns

Privacy (NIST) - Freedom from intrusion into the private life or affairs of an individual when that intrusion results from undue or illegal gathering and use of data about that individual

Common misconception: Privacy and Cybersecurity are not the the same things. However they are strongly linked, and good cybersecurity can help ensure privacy.

Why Does it Matter

This short article from The Guardian in 2018 gives a good idea of the scale and type of personal data that large tech companies collect on individuals. The type of data ranges from location, workout routines, emails, as well as everything you have searched for just to name a few. A detailed profile of an individual can be created using this information, e.g. Baby Prediction or Cambridge Analytica .

It would be fairly catastrophic if all this personal data fell into the wrongs hands as one could fall victim to fraud (financial or identity), stalking, discrimination,blackmail, mob justice, and potentially incarceration.

How can privacy be improved

On a basic level, there are three parties involved in improving privacy. Businesses, individuals, and governments.

Businesses

A design/ engineering technique known as Privacy by Design developed by Ann Cavoukian should be implemented. This outlines seven steps to ensure that user privacy is guaranteed. The most important ones, in my opinion is that privacy is the primary concern throughout the whole product development process and not just an afterthought.

The six remaining techniques are as follows:

  • Privacy is Proactive, Not Reactive Preparing for a breach before one happens, and mitigating losses.
  • Privacy as the Default Setting Only collecting necessary data for business functions. This reduces the damage when a breach occurs
  • Full Functionality – Positive-sum, not Zero-sum Implementation of privacy techniques does not harm the functionality of the business, but instead improves on it.
  • End-to-End Protection – Lifecycle Security Ensuring that data is private and secure througout it's lifespan of collection, storages, usage and disposal
  • Visibility and Transparency Individuals who have data collected on them are aware what their information is used for.
  • Respect for User Privacy – Keep it User-Centric Users have more control over their data and can easily opt out of data collection and usage practices.

Individuals

Ensuring privacy as an individual, like staying secure online, is fairly easy to do, yet again with vavrying degrees of difficulty.

Governments

Governments also play a key part in ensuring the privacy of it's citizens. This is achieved through up to date data protection legislation as well as it's enforcement.

At the moment any company in the UK that handles personal data is subject to the Data Protection Act of 2018, UK GDPR and potentially EU GDPR if they handle data of individuals in the EU. A link to the seven principles will be provided below, but GDPR states that personal data should be gathered in a way which is transparent, the users know what there data is being used for, data collection is minimal, information is accurate and not held for longer than it needs to be, and that the data is secure.
